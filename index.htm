Honours Thesis: An Analysis of Avian Population Trends in Metro Vancouver’s Stanley Park (download pdf)

Thanks to its location along the migration-heavy, highly-trafficked Pacific Flyway, Stanley Park is able to remain a biodiverse haven for bird watchers even with its proximity to the urbanized downtown region of Vancouver. To better protect such a biodiverse population (of both flora and fauna), Stanley Park Ecology Society (SPES) has established a variety of monitoring programs. Past results has suggested that avian species that are more habituated to disturbances (e.g. off-trail biking, recreational watercrafts) may increase in abundance, while the less-habituated would become less prevalent. To examine this, I performed an analysis on the avian population of the Park using data from four sites - Beaver Lake, Lost Lagoon, Devonian Park, and the Seawall - collected by SPES between 2001 and 2002 and between 2005 and 2016. My results indicated that, during the examined period, the general population of all sites had experienced temporal trends in abundance and richness but not diversity (quantified by the Simpson’s Index of Diversity), that disturbance-adapted birds did not always experience stability or increasing abundance (although their richnesses were mostly stable), and that the birds less adapted to disturbances did not always experience decreasing abundance. In fact, the birds that are less adapted were usually stable in abundance and richness. However, these results may not represent the true avian population of the Park due to the methodology. Future surveys should include another study site based entirely inside a forested area and should also be conducted at a higher frequency and with a consistent methodology.

Orphaned Wildlife Rehabilitation Society

Delta, Canada - Volunteer, Raptor Rehabilitation

October 2010 - June 2013

The Orphaned Wildlife Rehabilitation Society (OWL) is a rehabilitation centre that takes in sick and injured birds of prey from the surrounding cities. They also organize programs that bring raptors (who are already acclimated to humans) to various schools and events in order to educate the public on these birds. It was at one of these school presentations where my love for owls (and eventually other birds) was stoked, subsequently leading to my volunteering at OWL. There, I helped feed the raptors and other general tasks (e.g. laundry). Additionally, I was able to learn more about them while also becoming more comfortable with actual wild animals.
